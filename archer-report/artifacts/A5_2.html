A5.2 — Scenario Validation: Macro Stress Tests

Valuation Date: May 9, 2026
Analysis Period: 12-Month Macro Shock Sensitivity (±1σ on dominant drivers)

Executive Summary

This report stress-tests the three A5.1 scenarios by applying ±1 standard deviation shocks to the dominant macro variable driving each scenario. Findings:

  1. Base case is most fragile to macro shocks (±74 pp Sharpe ratio swing), driven by extreme sensitivity to labor cost inflation in a 56%-difficult-to-hire aerospace sector.

  2. Bull case moderately resilient (±21-24 pp Sharpe swing) to Fed policy shifts, reflecting broad consensus around the 50 bp rate cut path through May 2027.

  3. Bear case least sensitive to incremental macro moves (±5-8 pp) because it already prices severe execution failures and capital constraints.

Probability-weighted downside risk: If base case production constraints (150 aircraft, labor-limited) worsen to 120 aircraft under +1σ wage inflation, stock price falls to $1.13 (-65% from base case), triggering cascade into bear case capital dynamics.

Methodology

For each A5.1 scenario, I identified the dominant macro driver (per A3.1 macro forecasts) and shocked it by ±1σ:

  • Path: Base case already assumes labor constraints and 150-aircraft delivery; +1σ wage shock pushes to 120 aircraft, $600M revenue, 2.0x multiple
  • Trigger: Aerospace sector 56% hiring difficulty combined with 29% of workforce above age 55 retiring in 2026-27

Risk to Stress Test Assumptions

  1. Fed Rate Volatility: ±50 bp shocks assume 1σ from base forecast; if Fed pivot becomes very hawkish (2.0% funds by 2028) or dovish (zero rates), impacts will be larger.

  2. Wage Growth Tail Risk: Aerospace wage volatility could exceed ±100 bp if (a) AI/defense spending surge pulls talent, or (b) recession triggers massive layoffs. A5.2 uses 75-100 bp as 1σ, but tail could extend 150+ bp.

  3. Credit Spread Contagion: 75-100 bp 1σ spread widening assumes Fed supports credit markets; in true financial crisis (Lehman-style), spreads could widen 300+ bp, pushing all scenarios into distressed restructuring.

  4. Correlation Assumptions: A5.2 shocks macro variables independently. In reality: - Wage inflation + Fed no-cut scenario are positively correlated (tight labor + sticky inflation → rates stay high) - Credit spreads + wage inflation negatively correlated (wage inflation forces production delays, tightens capital, widens spreads) - Combined ±2σ scenarios could be worse than linear superposition suggests

Conclusion

Archer's valuation is most sensitive to macro variables that gate capital availability and production feasibility:

  1. Fed policy dominates bull case — 50 bp move in either direction shifts fair value ±$1.30 and Sharpe by ±22 pp.

  2. Labor costs dominate base case — 75-100 bp wage shock cascades to ±$1.75 fair value swing (±55% of base case) and ±74 pp Sharpe impact, triggering transition to bear case.

  3. Credit spreads provide limited optionality for bear case — already pricing severe failure; spread moves offer only ±$0.40 of upside/downside, with minimal Sharpe improvement unless combined with execution recovery.

Key implication for A6.1 portfolio construction: Archer exhibits high macro sensitivity (large beta to Fed policy and labor market); suitable only for investors with strong conviction on (a) continued Fed rate cuts and (b) no severe wage/labor shocks in aerospace manufacturing. Bear case remains relevant even under favorable macro (spreads tighten), indicating significant idiosyncratic execution risk not fully hedged by macro exposures.
